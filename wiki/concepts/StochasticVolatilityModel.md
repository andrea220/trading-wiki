# Stochastic Volatility Model

A **stochastic volatility (SV) model** extends [[GeometricBrownianMotion]] by making the volatility (or variance) itself a stochastic process. This introduces a second source of randomness and allows the model to generate the [[ImpliedVolatility]] smile and skew observed in real markets.

---

## Motivation

Under [[BlackScholesModel]], volatility σ is a constant. Real markets show:
1. **Volatility clustering**: periods of high vol are followed by high vol; calm periods by calm.
2. **Mean reversion**: vol tends to revert toward a long-run level over time.
3. **Negative correlation** with equity prices: vol typically rises when stock prices fall (leverage effect / fear asymmetry).
4. **Smile/skew in implied vols**: the IV surface cannot be explained by a single σ.

SV models address all four. The price: the market is no longer complete (two Brownian motions, one risky asset), so a unique risk-neutral measure requires an additional assumption — a **market price of volatility risk**.

---

## General Structure

A two-factor SV model for an asset S and its variance v:

```
dS(t) = r S(t) dt + √v(t) S(t) dW^Q_S(t)
dv(t) = μ_v(v(t), t) dt + σ_v(v(t), t) dW^Q_v(t)
dW_S · dW_v = ρ dt
```

Key design choices:
- **Variance dynamics** `μ_v, σ_v`: determines mean reversion speed, vol-of-vol, and positivity guarantees.
- **Correlation ρ**: the primary driver of the **skew** in implied vols. Negative ρ (typical for equities) makes the left tail fatter → downward IV skew.

---

## Key Models

| Model | Variance process | Tractability |
|---|---|---|
| Hull-White (1987) | Log-normal (no mean reversion) | Semi-analytic |
| Stein-Stein / Schöbel-Zhu | Ornstein-Uhlenbeck (can go negative) | Analytic ChF |
| **[[HestonModel]]** (1993) | CIR (mean-reverting, non-negative) | Closed-form ChF via Riccati ODEs |
| SABR | Log-normal stochastic vol | Approx. formula for swaption vol |
| Rough Heston | Fractional BM-driven variance | Integral equations (recent research) |

The [[HestonModel]] is the industry standard for equity options and the central model in Oosterlee & Grzelak.

---

## Implied Volatility Generated by SV Models

An SV model with ρ < 0 generates a **skew**: OTM puts appear more expensive (higher IV) than OTM calls. The shape depends on parameters:

- **ρ (correlation)**: controls skew slope. More negative ρ → steeper downward skew.
- **κ (mean reversion speed)**: faster reversion → flatter surface at long maturities.
- **η (vol-of-vol)**: higher η → more smile curvature.
- **v₀ (initial variance)**: controls the overall level of short-term IV.
- **θ (long-run variance)**: controls the long-term level of IV.

---

## Completeness and the Market Price of Vol Risk

With two BMs (asset and vol) and one traded asset, the market is **incomplete**. The risk-neutral dynamics for v require specifying the **market price of volatility risk** λ(v, S, t). Different choices of λ give different risk-neutral measures and hence different option prices. In practice, λ is often set to zero or absorbed into the mean-reversion parameters (the distinction between P and Q parameters is then unclear).
